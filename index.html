Choose Your Puffer (Beginner‑friendly picks)

Pea Puffer (Freshwater)

Size: ~1 inch • Temperament: Feisty • Min tank: 5–10 gal solo.

  • 100% freshwater; no salt needed.
  • Best solo or species‑only; can fin‑nip tankmates.
  • Dense plants and line‑of‑sight breaks reduce stress.

Green Spotted Puffer (Brackish → Marine)

Size: 4–6 in • Temperament: Nippy • Min tank: 30+ gal.

  • Starts brackish; needs higher salinity as it matures.
  • Powerful filtration; messy eater.
  • Usually kept singly to avoid aggression.

Porcupine Puffer (Marine)

Size: up to 12–18 in • Temperament: Puppy‑like but big • Min tank: 125+ gal.

  • Saltwater only; robust skimming & biological filtration.
  • Very large bio‑load—plan big from day one.
  • Will rearrange decor and beg for food 😄.

💡 Pro tip: research the adult size, not just juvenile size. Puffers outgrow “starter” tanks fast.

Tank Setup & Water Parameters

Core Gear

  • Appropriately sized tank with lid (jump risk!)
  • Heater: stable temp (species vary; many like 76–80°F / 24–27°C)
  • Filter with gentle flow; oversized for messy eaters
  • Test kits: ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, pH (and salinity for brackish/marine)
  • Hardscape & plants: hiding spots break sight lines

Water Basics

  • Cycle the tank first. Zero ammonia & nitrite before adding fish.
  • Nitrate: aim <20–40 ppm (lower for sensitive species).
  • pH & hardness: match species and keep it stable.
  • Brackish/marine: use marine salt mix; measure salinity with a refractometer.

Never “test” a puffer with cycling. Do a fishless cycle and add slowly.

Daily & Weekly Care

Daily

  • Quick health scan: clear eyes, smooth breathing, active but not frantic.
  • Feed appropriate foods (see below); remove uneaten bits.
  • Top off evaporated water (freshwater for brackish/marine tanks).

Weekly

  • Water change 20–40% depending on bioload.
  • Gravel vac/light sand stir; clean glass.
  • Test water; log results to spot trends.

Diet & Enrichment

Puffers are carnivores with strong beaks that keep growing. They need crunchy foods to wear teeth down.

  • Staples: snails, shrimp, krill, bloodworms, clams/mussels (appropriately sized).
  • Crunch factor: offer hard‑shelled foods regularly to prevent overgrowth.
  • Variety: rotate foods; avoid exclusively “soft” frozen fare.
  • Enrichment: tongs feeding, scatter feeding, safe decor to explore.

Avoid feeder fish; they can introduce disease and poor nutrition.

Health & Safety

  • Acclimate slowly; avoid rapid shifts in salinity or temperature.
  • Quarantine new livestock when possible.
  • Toxin note: Some puffers carry tetrodotoxin internally—admire, don’t handle.
  • Stress: A puffed puffer is stressed; focus on calm, clean water.
